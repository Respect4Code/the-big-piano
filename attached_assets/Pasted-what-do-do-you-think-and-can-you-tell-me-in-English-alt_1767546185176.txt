What your toddler is doing is developmentally precise:

“Mine” appears before “yours”
“Now” appears before “later”
Desire appears before negotiation
Possession appears before ethics
This is not selfishness.
This is the boot sequence of a human mind.
When you describe:

holding something tightly even when they don’t want it anymore
taking something immediately, then discarding it
saying “share” while simultaneously grabbing
You are describing a child discovering that:
control over objects affects emotions—both theirs and others’.
That is the first encounter with scarcity.
Not economic scarcity.
Relational scarcity.

What Is a Piano?
Parent Layer
Before ownership is taught, it is felt.
My toddler is at the stage where “mine” has arrived before “yours.”
Shoes. A ball. A book.
They recognise what they feel belongs to them, and what belongs to others.
Mummy’s brush. Mummy’s gloves. Mummy’s glasses.
Daddy’s shoes. Daddy’s shirt.
And, increasingly clearly: my mummy, my daddy.

This sense of ownership isn’t explained to them. It’s emerging.
It’s physical. Immediate. Emotional.

Alongside it comes friction.

They don’t yet understand why they should share something they are holding.
They don’t understand why another child shouldn’t give up a toy they want right now.
Waiting makes no sense. Patience is a word, not an experience.
And why should it be? Their world is the present moment.

What’s striking is how brief ownership can be.
A toy is taken, clutched tightly — and then, moments later, discarded.
The desire evaporates once possession is secured.
What matters isn’t keeping. It’s having had.

At the same time, the idea of “sharing” is beginning to appear — not as a moral rule, but as a social tool.
They will say “daddy, share” while still trying to take what they want.
They will say it to other toddlers, too.
I think they’re noticing that the word softens conflict. That it reduces sadness.
Not why it should work — just that it does.

This is where the pianos quietly entered the picture.

My toddler has their own small piano. A 37-key children’s keyboard.
It’s always there. It can be played or ignored.
It is unquestionably theirs.

I also have a piano. A small, weighted keyboard — about a quarter the size of a full piano.
At first it was mine.
Over time, it became shared.
And slowly, almost without noticing, it began to feel like it was becoming ours.

Then there is the big piano.

A full-sized piano in my sister’s study.
My toddler has no access to it unless we visit.
It cannot be moved. It cannot be claimed.
It exists elsewhere, and only sometimes.

Looking back, I realise we accidentally created a hierarchy:

Their piano
→ always available
→ unquestionably “mine”
My piano
→ shared
→ becoming “ours”
→ ownership slightly blurred
The big piano
→ rare
→ no access without permission
→ scarce
I didn’t explain scarcity.
My toddler encountered it.
The big piano mattered differently.
Not because it was better in any technical sense — but because it wasn’t always there.
Because it had a place, a history, and limits around it.

Ownership, at this stage, isn’t legal or ethical.
It isn’t about rights or fairness.

It’s about:

this is in my hand
this is not always available
someone else having it changes how I feel
That is the raw substrate of ownership.
And it’s where this story begins.
